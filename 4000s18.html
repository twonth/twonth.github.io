Semester-to-date summary (reverse chronological order)
  • 4/25 The "constructible numbers" game. All integers are constructible. Rational numbers are constructible. $\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{3}$ and $\sqrt{2}$ are not constructible. A criterion for constructibility (no proof); $\sqrt[3]{2}$ is not constructible.
  • 4/23 Definition of the degree of a field extension. $[F:K]=1 \Longleftrightarrow F=K$. If $L/K/F$ is a tower of fields, then $[L:F]$ is finite if and only if $[L:K]$ and $[K:F]$ are finite; in that case, $[L:F]=[L:K]\cdot [K:F]$. If $K/F$ is a field extension and $\alpha \in K$ is the root of the irreducible polynomial $p(x) \in F[x]$, then $[F[\alpha]:F] = \deg{p(x)}$.
  • 4/18 gcds in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ and primes in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Euclid's lemma in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. If $p$ is prime (in $\mathbb{Z}$) and $p\equiv 1\pmod{4}$, then $p$ is not prime in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Every prime $p\equiv 1\pmod{4}$ is a sum of two squares.
  • 4/16 Constructing a splitting field for $x^3-2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. Statement of Fermat's two-square theorem: an odd prime $p$ is a sum of two squares $\Longleftrightarrow$ $p=2$ or $p\equiv 1\pmod{4}$. Proof that primes $p\equiv 3\pmod{4}$ are not sums of two squares. Lemma that if $p\equiv 1\pmod{4}$, there is an integer $x$ with $p \mid x^2+1$.
  • 4/13 Three successively stronger theorems: Let $F$ be a field and let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be nonconstant; there is a field $K \supset F$ containing a root of $f(x)$. Let $F$ be a field and let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be nonconstant; there is a field $K$ containing $F$ over which $f(x)$ splits. Let $F$ be a field and let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be nonconstant; there is a splitting field for $f(x)$ over $F$. Examples.
  • 4/11 Proof of the fundamental homomorphism theorem. Proof that $\mathbb{Z}_{mn} \cong \mathbb{Z}_{m} \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ when $\gcd(m,n)=1$. Proof that if $F$ is a subfield of $K$, and $\alpha \in K$ is the root of an irreducible polynomial $p(x) \in F[x]$, then $F[x]/p(x)F[x] \cong F[\alpha]$. Construction of a field $K$ containing $\mathbb{Z}_2$ in which $f(x)=x^2+x+1$ has a root.
  • 4/9 Detailed proof that complex conjugation is an isomorphism of $\mathbb{C}$ with itself. Definition of direct product of two rings. Statement that $\mathbb{Z}_{mn} \cong \mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ whenever $\gcd(m,n)=1$. Proof that $\mathbb{Z}_4\not\cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$. Statement of the fundamental homomorphism theorem.
  • 4/4 Examples of quotient rings $R/I$, where $R=F[x]$ ($F$ a field) and $I = f(x) F[x]$. Definition of an isomorphism from a ring $R$ to a ring $S$.
  • 4/2 Definition of the "principal ideal" $rR$. Every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is principal, and the same for $F[x]$ and $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Construction of the quotient ring $R/I$.
  • 3/30 Examples of homomorphisms. Definition of $\ker \phi$. A homomorphism $\phi$ is one-to-one $\Longleftrightarrow$ $\ker \phi = \{0\}$. $\ker \phi$ contains $0$, is closed under addition, and absorbs multiplication. Definition of an ideal of a commutative ring $R$. Every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is of the form $m\mathbb{Z}$ for some integer $m$.
  • 3/28 Eisenstein's irreducibility criterion, with examples. Definition of a homomorphism between rings $R$ and $S$. A homomorphism $\phi\colon R\to S$ maps $0_R$ to $0_S$ and carries $R$ to a subring $\phi(R)$ of $S$.
  • 3/26 Statement of Gauss's lemma. Proving irreducibility by the method of undetermined coefficients (specifically, the example of $x^4+1$). Testing irreducibility by reduction modulo $p$. Discussion of Midterm #2.
  • 3/21 Further discussion of splitting fields. Rational root test.
  • 3/19 What it means to say $F$ is a subfield of $K$. If $F$ is a subfield of $K$ and $\alpha \in K$ is the root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $F[\alpha]$ is a field. What it means to say that $f(x) \in F[x]$ splits in $K$. What it means to say that $K$ is a splitting field of $f(x) \in F[x]$. Examples.
  • 3/9 Definition of $F[\alpha]$, where $F$ and $K$ are fields with $F\subset K$, and $\alpha \in K$. $F[\alpha]$ is a ring with $F \subset F[\alpha] \subset K$. If $\alpha$ is the root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $F[\alpha]$ is a field (proof postponed to post-Spring break). Examples. Proof of the following lemma: If $\alpha$ is the root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $\alpha$ is the root of an irreducible polynomial in $F[x]$.
  • 3/7 If $f(x) \in F[x]$ has degree $2$ or $3$, then $f(x)$ is irreducible if and only if $f(x)$ has no root in $F$. The definition of the gcd in $F[x]$. Computing the gcd via Euclid's algorithm. Euclid's lemma in $F[x]$. Statement of the Unique Factorization Theorem in $F[x]$.
  • 3/5 If $R$ is a domain, then $\deg{fg} = \deg{f} + \deg{g}$ for every pair of nonzero polynomials $f(x),g(x) \in R[x]$. The division algorithm in $F[x]$ with $F$ a field. The remainder theorem and the root-factor theorem in $F[x]$. Definition of an irreducible polynomial and first examples.
  • 3/2 The cubic formula in action. Start of discussion of $R[x]$ (polynomials over $R$).
  • 2/28 Finding roots of linear, quadratic, and cubic polynomials.
  • 2/26 Postponement of Midterm #2 until after Spring Break. Multiplication of complex numbers in polar form. Existence of $n$ $n$th roots of a nonzero complex number. The $n$th roots of $1$ give the vertices of a regular $n$-gon. Definition of a polynomial over the complex numbers.
  • 2/23 Construction of the complex numbers. Definition of the complex conjugate $\bar{z}$, and of $\mathrm{Re}(z)$, $\mathrm{Im}(z)$, and $|z|$. Definition of polar form.
  • 2/21 Further discussion of the real numbers, including a proof of the Archimedean principle and the statement that the rationals are dense in $\mathbb{R}$. Discussion of $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]$ as a field sitting between $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{R}$.
  • 2/19 Definition of an ordered domain. $\mathbb{Q}$ has holes: there is no $x \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $x^2=2$. Diagnosis of the problem as being a failure of the "completeness axiom", namely that every bounded increasing sequence converges. Statement that there is a unique ordered field $\mathbb{R}$ (the real numbers) containing $\mathbb{Q}$, preserving the operations and ordering of $\mathbb{Q}$, and where the completeness axiom holds. Explanation of why $\sqrt{2}$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$. Passed back Exam #1.
  • 2/16 Midterm #1
  • 2/14 Proof that the map $n\mapsto n/1$ is injective and operation-preserving. Definition of $\mathbb{Q}^{+}$. The Archimedean principle for rational numbers. Between any two rational numbers there is a third.
  • 2/12 Definition of the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. Addition and multiplication (including proofs these are well-defined). $\mathbb{Q}$ is a commutative ring. $\mathbb{Q}$ is a field. What it means to say that $\mathbb{Z}$ "lives inside" $\mathbb{Q}$.
  • 2/9 Definition of a unit in a ring. Proof that $\bar{a}$ is a unit in $\mathbb{Z}_m$ if and only if $\gcd(a,m)=1$. $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is a field when $m$ is prime. Every finite integral domain is a field. Statement of the binomial theorem for a general commutative ring $R$.
  • 2/7 Definition of a ring. Definition of the zero ring; proof that in the zero ring, $0$ is the only element. Addition and multiplication tables for $\mathbb{Z}_3$ vs $\mathbb{Z}_4$. Definition of an integral domain. Proof that for a positive integer $m$, the ring $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is the zero ring if and only if $m=1$. Proof that if $m>1$ is not prime, then $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is not an integral domain. Proof that if $m>1$ is prime, then $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is an integral domain. Definition of a field. Every field is an integral domain.
  • 2/5 Another example of the Chinese remainder theorem. The definition of $\mathbb{Z}_m$. The definition of $+$ and $\cdot$ for $\mathbb{Z}_m$, and a proof that these are not ``over-defined''. A spot check of the algebraic axioms for $\mathbb{Z}_m$.
  • 2/2 Vote to postpone Midterm #1. More examples of linear congruences. Chinese remainder theorem.
  • 1/31 Proof of Fermat's little theorem. If $\gcd(a,m)=1$, then there is an $a'$ with $aa' \equiv 1\pmod{m}$. If $\gcd(a,m)=1$, then the solutions to $ax\equiv b\pmod{m}$ are those $x \equiv a'b \pmod{m}$.
  • 1/29 Congruences mod $m$ can be added and multiplied. Every integer is congruent, modulo $m$, to exactly one one of $0,1,2,3,\dots,m-1$. Numerical example: $17^{5}$ and $17^{17}$ modulo $5$. Every integer is congruent to its sum of base-10 digits modulo $9$. Statement of Fermat's little theorem and the key lemma about binomial coefficients.
  • 1/26 Proof of the existence half of the unique factorization theorem. Euclid's lemma. The uniqueness half of the unique factorization theorem. Definition of congruence modulo $m$. Congruence modulo $m$ is an equivalence relation.
  • 1/24 Proof that when Euclid's algorithm terminates, the last nonzero remainder is the gcd. Proof that Euclid's algorithm always terminates. The common divisors of $a$ and $b$ are precisely the divisors of $\gcd(a,b)$. If $a\mid bc$ and $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime, then $a \mid c$. Statement of the theorem on unique factorization.
  • 1/22 Even and odd numbers. Division algorithm: statement and proof. Definition of $m$ divides $n$ (written $m\mid n$). Basic properties of the divides relation. Definition of the gcd. Description of Euclid's magic algorithm for computing the gcd.

Deduction of the Principle of Mathematical Induction from the Well Ordering Principle. Definition of exponents; statement of laws of exponents. Statement and proof of the binomial theorem.

Course synopsis

At this point in your mathematical career, you have accumulated a wealth of experience computing with integers, real numbers, and complex numbers. These items are so familiar to you that they may even have the appearance of being God-given --- as if understanding those objects is What Mathematics is All About.

Abstract algebra challenges this notion. Integers, real numbers, and complex numbers are indeed fantastic, but they are fantastic not because they are handed down from on high, but because they have a rich and (presumably) consistent theory with useful consequences. And given how useful these objects are, we are compelled to isolate (and consider in the abstract) their most important properties. Once we do so, we find that there are many other objects with the same sorts of interesting properties. For example, we will see that the integers are an example of what is called a ring, while the real numbers and complex numbers are fields. These more abstract-seeming objects are not just interesting in an intellectual let's-talk-about-this-over-coffee kind of way, but understanding them deeply often leads to a new understanding of the objects of original interest.

Let me give one concrete example: Some primes, like 5, can be written as a sum of two squares: $5 = 1^2 + 2^2$. And other primes, like 3, cannot. The question of when this is possible is a question about the integers. But our answer to this question --- and we will answer it by the end of the semester --- will require us to visit a totally different mathematically system, the ring of Gaussian integers.

  • Homework: 25%
  • Final exam: 30%

This class falls into the interactive lecture genre (not entirely unrelated to the practice of call and response in a liturgical context). What this means is that I intend to punctuate the lectures frequently with questions for you. For the show to go on, class participation is absolutely essential. Since you cannot participate in class if you are not present in class, your attendance is required. In particular, more than four unexcused absences may result in you being automagically withdrawn from the class. Of course, missing class is sometimes a necessity; keep me posted whenever you have a conflict and we should not have any issues.

Homework will be collected roughly once each week. As a general rule, late homework assignments are not accepted. Your lowest HW score will be dropped at the end of the semester.

All exams are closed book and closed notes.

Students enrolled in MATH 6000 will take the same exams as the students in MATH 4000 but will be assigned additional homework problems.

You are not only allowed, but encouraged to collaborate with your classmates on the homework assignments. The joy of mathematical discovery was meant to be shared! Having said that, collaboration does not mean copying. You may not copy solutions from a textbook, classmate, website, etcetera, and you must be the one to handwrite (or type) your solutions. By entering UGA, you have already agreed to abide by the UGA honor code described in detail at https://honesty.uga.edu/Academic-Honesty-Policy/. A good rule of thumb is that you should be able to explain any work you turn in to a hypothetical interrogator.
