Instructions

All problems are of equal weight (5 points). Please arrange your solutions in numerical order even if you do not solve them in that order. Show work and carefully justify/prove your assertions.

Problems

  1. For \(n \in \mathbb{Z}_+\) and \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), define

    \[ f_n(x) = \frac{x}{1 + n x^2}. \]

    1. Show that \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) converges uniformly on \(\mathbb{R}\) to a function \(f\), and determine \(f\).
    2. Is it true that \(f_n'(x) \to f'(x)\) for almost every \(x \in \mathbb{R}\)?
    3. Let \(X = \left\{x \in \mathbb{R} : \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n'(x) = f'(x)\right\}\). Is it true that \(f_n'(x) \to f'(x)\) uniformly on \(X\)?

  2. Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space and let \(f\) be a measurable function. Prove that \(f \in L^1(X)\) if and only if

    \[ \sum_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} 2^k \mu\!\left(\left\{x \in X : 2^k \le |f(x)| < 2^{k+1}\right\}\right) < \infty. \]

  3. Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a \(\sigma\)-finite measure space. Then, for \(f \in L^2(X)\) we have

    \[ \|f\|_{L^2(X)}^2 = 2 \int_0^\infty \alpha\,\mu(\{x \in X : |f(x)| > \alpha\})\,d\alpha. \]

    [Hint: Fubini's theorem may be useful.]

  4. Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be a Hilbert space.

    1. Let \(M_1 \le \mathcal{H}\) be a finite dimensional subspace and let \(S : M_1 \to \mathcal{H}\) be a linear operator. Prove that \(S\) is a bounded operator, that is there is a constant \(C>0\) such that \(\|Sf\| \le C\|f\|\) for all \(f \in M_1\).
    2. Let \(f_1,\ldots,f_n \in \mathcal{H}\) and \(g_1,\ldots,g_n \in \mathcal{H}\) such that \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\) are linearly independent. Prove that there exists a bounded linear operator \(T : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) such that \(Tf_k = g_k\) for all \(1 \le k \le n\).

      Hint for part (b): You can use the orthogonal projection to the subspace spanned by the vectors \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\) in constructing the operator \(T\), or equivalently, you can use the orthogonal decomposition of \(\mathcal{H}\) into this subspace and its orthogonal complement.

  5. Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space. Let \((f_n)_{n=1}^\infty, (g_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be two sequences of measurable functions, and assume that \(f_n \to f\) and \(g_n \to g\) in measure, for some measurable functions \(f\) and \(g\).

    1. If \(f, g\) are bounded show that \(f_n g_n \to fg\) in measure.
    2. If \(\mu(X) < \infty\), then \(f_n g_n \to fg\) in measure.

      Hint for part (b): reduce to part (a).

  6. Let \(\mathbb{T}\) be a torus and let \(f \in L^2(\mathbb{T}), k \in L^1(\mathbb{T})\).

    1. Show that

    \[ Tf(x) = \int_{\mathbb{T}} f(x-y)k(y)\,dy \]

    converges for almost every \(x \in \mathbb{T}\).

    1. Show that \(\|Tf\|_2 \le \|k\|_1\|f\|_2\).
    2. Compute the Fourier series of \(Tf\), that is \(\widehat{Tf}(m)\) for \(m \in \mathbb{Z}\), where

    \[ \widehat{Tf}(m) = \int_{\mathbb{T}} Tf(x)e^{-2\pi i x m}\,dx. \]

    1. Show that \(\|\hat{k}\|_\infty \le \|k\|_1\) and

    \[ \sup_{\|f\|_2 \le 1}\|Tf\|_2 = \|\hat{k}\|_\infty. \]

August, 2025

Instructions

State clearly which theorem you are using. Conclusions in one problem in this exam may be used for another without proof. Each problem is worth 10 points.

Notation: \(m\) is the Lebesgue measure on the space \(\mathbb{R}^n\), and so is \(dx\).

Problems

  1. Let \(f\) be an \(\mathbb{R}\)-valued measurable function on \([0,1]\). Put \(A := f^{-1}(\mathbb{Z})\), and for \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), \(f_n(x) := [\cos(\pi f(x))]^{2n}\). Show that \(A\) is measurable and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f_n(x)\,dx = m(A)\).

  2. For \(x \neq 0\), define \(f(x)\) by the series

    \[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-n|x|} \]

    1. Let \(d > 0\). Show that for \(x \in (-\infty,-d) \cup (d,\infty)\), the series converges uniformly and \(f(x)\) is uniformly continuous.

    2. Show that for \(x \in (-\infty,0) \cup (0,\infty)\), the series is not uniformly convergent and \(f(x)\) is not uniformly continuous.

  3. (10 points) Let \(f\) be a bounded real valued function on \([a,b]\), Assume that

    \[ \sup \left\{ \int_{[a,b]} \phi \, dm : \phi \text{ is simple, } \phi \leq f \right\} = \inf \left\{ \int_{[a,b]} \psi \, dm : \psi \text{ is simple, } f \leq \psi \right\} \]

    Show that \(f\) is Lebesgue measurable.

  4. Let \(f\) be Lebesgue integrable on \((0,a)\) and \(g(x) = \int_x^a \frac{f(t)}{t}\,dt\). Show that \(g\) is measurable and integrable on \((0,a)\) and \(\int_0^a g(x)\,dx = \int_0^a f(x)\,dx\).

  5. Let \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\). Define

    \[ F(x,r) := \frac{1}{r}\int_{[x-r,x+r]} f(y)\,dy, \qquad (x,r) \in \mathbb{R}\times(0,\infty). \]

    Show that \(F\) is continuous in \((x,r)\).

    1. For \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), \(t \in \mathbb{R}\), define \(\tau_t(f)(x) = f(x-t)\). Show that \(t \mapsto \tau_t(f)\) is a continuous map from \(\mathbb{R}\) to \(L^1(\mathbb{R})\).

    2. Let \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\) and \(g\) a bounded measurable function. Show that \(h = f * g\) is uniformly continuous, where as usual, \(h(y) := (f * g)(y) := \int_{\mathbb{R}} f(y-x)g(x)\,dx\).

January, 2025

Instructions

State clearly which theorem you are using. Conclusions in one problem in this exam may be used for another without proof. Each problem is worth 10 points.

Notation: \(m\) is the Lebesgue measure on the space \(\mathbb{R}^n\), and so is \(dx\).

Problems

  1. Let \(f\) be a continuous complex valued function on \([0,1]\). Show that

    \[ \|f\|_{\infty} = \sup \left\{ |\lambda| : \lambda \in \mathbb{C},\ m\!\left(f^{-1}(B(\lambda,\varepsilon))\right) > 0 \text{ for every } \varepsilon > 0 \right\}, \]

    where \(B(\lambda,\varepsilon) \subset \mathbb{C}\) is the open disc of radius \(\varepsilon\) centered at \(\lambda\).

  2. Show that Lebesgue measurable functions \(f_n\) defined on \([a,b]\) converge in measure to \(f\) if and only if

    \[ \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_a^b \frac{|f_n-f|}{1+|f_n-f|}\,dx = 0. \]

  3. Show that if \(f\) is Riemann integrable on \([a,b]\) and \(f(x)=0\) for \(x\in \mathbb{Q}\cap[a,b]\), then

    \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = 0. \]

  4. Let \(f_n \in L^2([0,1])\) be a sequence that is bounded in norm \(\|\cdot\|_2\) and \(f_n \rightarrow 0\) a.e.

    Show that \(\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\|f_n\|_1 = 0\).

    Hint: Use Egoroff and Cauchy-Schwarz.

  5. Show that for every \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), there exists a sequence of continuous functions \(g_n\) with compact supports such that \(\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\|g_n-f\|_1 = 0\).

  6. Let \(f \in L^1[0,1]\). Prove that

    \[ \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_0^1 f(x)|\sin nx|\,dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx \]

    Hint: start with the easiest function \(f\) first.

August, 2024

Instructions

State clearly which theorem you are using. You may quote the conclusion of a part of one problem for another (part of the same or different problem) even if you have not proved the quoted one. Each of the six problems is worth 10 points.

Notation: \(m\) is the Lebesgue measure on the set \(\mathbb{R}\) of reals, and so is \(dx\).

Problems

  1. Show that the function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{1 - e^{-x^2}}\) is uniformly continuous outside \((-\delta,\delta)\) for every \(\delta > 0\), but it fails to be uniformly continuous on \(\mathbb{R}\).

  2. Given a sequence of measurable sets \(A_1, A_2, \ldots\) in \([0,1]\), define

    \[ \limsup A_n := \bigcap_{k=1}^{\infty}\bigcup_{n=k}^{\infty} A_n, \qquad \liminf A_n := \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}\bigcap_{n=k}^{\infty} A_n. \]

    Show that

    \[ \liminf A_n \subset \limsup A_n \]

    and that

    \[ \limsup m(A_n) \le m(\limsup A_n), \qquad m(\liminf A_n) \le \liminf m(A_n). \]

  3. Let \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) be a continuously differentiable function. Recall that a point \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) is a critical point of \(f\) if \(f'(x)=0\), and a point \(y \in \mathbb{R}\) is a critical value of \(f\) if \(y=f(x)\) for some critical point \(x\). Prove that the set of all critical values of \(f\) has Lebesgue measure zero.

    Suggestion: Consider the Mean Value Theorem.

  4. Let \(E\) be a Lebesgue measurable set with \(m(E) < \infty\). For each \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), let \(E + x = \{y + x : y \in E\}\), and define

    \[ f(x) = m\bigl(E \cap (E + x)\bigr). \]

    Show that

    1. \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), and
    2. \(\lim_{|x|\to\infty} f(x) = 0\).

  5. For \(t \in (0,\infty)\), define \(f(t) := \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-t x^2}\,dx\). Show that

    1. \(f'(t)\) exists, and
    2. \(f'(t)\) is continuous.

  6. For the Lebesgue measure,

    1. define \(L^\infty(\mathbb{R})\) and \(\|f\|_\infty\) for \(f \in L^\infty(\mathbb{R})\), and
    2. show that \(L^\infty(\mathbb{R})\) is a Banach space.

January, 2024

Instructions

State clearly which theorem you are using. You may also quote the conclusions of one problem in this exam for another even if you do not prove the quoted ones.

Notation: \(m\) is the Lebesgue measure on the set \(\mathbb{R}\) of reals, and so is \(dx\).

Problems

  1. Show that the function

    \[ f(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^x}, \quad x>1 \]

    has a continuous derivative \(f'(x)\) given by

    \[ f'(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}-\frac{\ln n}{n^x}, \quad x>1 \]

  2. Let \(f:[1,\infty)\to\mathbb{R}\) be continuous, bounded function. Prove that

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{1}^{\infty} f(t) n t^{-n-1}\,dt = f(1). \]

    [Hint: Use the special case when \(f(x)=1\).]

  3. Let \(\phi\) be a continuously differentiable function on \(\mathbb{R}\) such that \(\phi(x)>0\) if \(|x|<1\) and \(\phi(x)=0\) if \(|x|\geq 1\), and \(\int_{\mathbb{R}}\phi(x)\,dx=1\). Put \(K_n(x):=n\phi(nx)\). Then (no proof required) \(\int_{\mathbb{R}}K_n(y)\,dy=1\) and \(K_n(x)=0\) if \(|x|\geq \frac{1}{n}\). Recall that

    \[ f*K_n(x)=K_n*f(x)=\int_{\mathbb{R}}K_n(x-y)f(y)\,dy. \]

    Prove the following:

    1. If \(f\in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), then \(f*K_n\in L^1(\mathbb{R})\) and has a continuous derivative for each \(n\).
    2. If \(f\in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), \(f*K_n\) converges to \(f\) in \(L^1\) as \(n\to\infty\).

  4. Let \(f\in L^1(\mathbb{R})\). Define a linear transform \(T_f\) on \(L^1(\mathbb{R})\) by \(T_f(g)=f*g,\ g\in L^1(\mathbb{R})\). Show that (i) \(\sup_{\|g\|_1\leq 1}\|T_f(g)\|_1=\|f\|_1\), and (ii) \(T_f=0\) if and only if \(f=0\) in \(L^1(\mathbb{R})\).

  5. Let \(f\) be a continuous complex valued function on \([0,1]\). Show that

    \[ f([0,1])=\left\{\lambda\in\mathbb{C}: m\!\left(f^{-1}(B(\lambda,\varepsilon))\right)>0 \text{ for every } \varepsilon>0\right\}, \]

    where \(B(\lambda,\varepsilon)\subset\mathbb{C}\) is the open disc of radius \(\varepsilon\) centered at \(\lambda\).

  6. (1) Prove the following Riemann-Lebesgue identities for any \(f\in L^1([-\pi,\pi])\):

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\cos nt\,dt=0, \qquad \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\sin nt\,dt=0. \]

    (2) Deduce the following: for any measurable set \(E\subset[-\pi,\pi]\) and any sequence \(s_n\) of real numbers,

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_E \cos^2(nt+s_n)\,dt = m(E)/2. \]

Fall 2023

Instructions

The five problems on this exam have equal weighting. To receive full credit give complete justification for all assertions by either citing known theorems or giving arguments from first principles.

Problems

  1. Let \(f_n(x) = \frac{n x^2}{n^3 + x^3}\).

    1. Prove that \(f_n\) converge uniformly to \(0\) on \([0,M]\) for any \(M > 0\), but does not converge uniformly to \(0\) on \([0,\infty)\).

    2. Prove that the series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)\) defines a continuous function on \([0,\infty)\).

  2. Let \((X,\mathcal{A})\) be a measurable space and \(\mu\) is a non-negative set function on \(\mathcal{A}\) that is finitely additive with \(\mu(\emptyset)=0\). Recall that such a set function is said to be continuous from below if

    \[ \mu\!\left(\bigcup_j A_j\right)=\lim_{j\to\infty}\mu(A_j) \text{ whenever } A_j \text{ is an increasing sequence of sets in } \mathcal{A}. \]

    Prove that

    \[ \mu \text{ is a measure } \Longleftrightarrow \mu \text{ is continuous from below.} \]

  3. Prove that

    \[ 1-\frac{x^2}{2}\leq \cos x \leq e^{-x^2/2} \]

    for all \(|x|\leq 1\) and conclude from this that

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{|x|\leq 1}(\cos x)^n\,dx=1 \]

    Hint: You may use without proof that \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\pi x^2}\,dx = 1\).

  4. Let \(a,b>0\). Prove that

    \[ \int_{[0,1]\times[0,1]} \frac{1}{x^a+y^b}\,dm_2(x,y) < \infty \Longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b} > 1 \]

    where \(m_2\) denotes Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

    Hint: One possible approach would be to consider separately the regions where \(x^a \leq y^b\) and \(x^a > y^b\).

  5. Let \(f_k\to f\) a.e. on \(\mathbb{R}\) with \(\sup_k \|f_k\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R})} < \infty\). Prove that \(f\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) and that

    \[ \lim_{k\to\infty}\int_{\mathbb{R}} f_k g = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f g \]

    for all \(g\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\).

    Hint: First consider functions \(g\) supported on sets of finite measure and use Egorov's Theorem.

Solutions

Problem 1

    1. Fix \(M>0\). Since

      \[ \left|\frac{n x^2}{n^3+x^3}\right| \leq \frac{x^2}{n^2} \leq \frac{M^2}{n^2} \qquad \forall x\in[0,M], \]

      we have

      \[ \sup_{x\in[0,M]} |f_n(x)-0| \leq \frac{M^2}{n^2}\to 0 \qquad \text{as } n\to\infty. \]

    2. Take \(x=n\). Then

      \[ \sup_{x\in[0,\infty)}\left|\frac{n x^2}{n^3+x^3}\right| \geq \frac{n^3}{n^3+n^3} = \frac12, \]

      so \(\sup_{x\in[0,\infty)} |f_n(x)-0|\not\to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\).

  2. In light of (i) above, we know \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n\) does not converge uniformly on \([0,\infty)\), but it does converge uniformly on \([0,M]\) for every \(M>0\).

    Since

    \[ \left|\frac{n x^2}{n^3+x^3}\right|\leq \frac{M^2}{n^2} \qquad \forall x\in[0,M], \]

    and \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{M^2}{n^2}<\infty\), the Weierstrass M-test gives uniform convergence of \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)\) on \([0,M]\).

    Thus \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)\) defines a continuous function on \([0,M]\) for any \(M>0\), hence a continuous function on \([0,\infty)\).

Problem 2

\(\Rightarrow\): Assume \(\mu\) is a measure, and let \(A_1\subseteq A_2\subseteq \cdots\) be an increasing sequence in \(\mathcal{A}\). Set \(A_0=\emptyset\) and \(E_j=A_j\setminus A_{j-1}\). Then the \(E_j\) are pairwise disjoint and \(\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty} E_j=\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty} A_j\). By countable additivity,

\[ \mu\!\left(\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty} A_j\right) =\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty} E_j\right) =\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\mu(E_j) =\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\mu(E_j) =\lim_{n\to\infty}\mu(A_n). \]

\(\Leftarrow\): Assume \(\mu\) is continuous from below. Let \(E_1,E_2,\ldots\) be a countable collection of pairwise disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{A}\), and define \(A_k=\bigcup_{j=1}^{k}E_j\). Then \(A_k\) is increasing and \(\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}A_k=\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}E_j\). Hence

\[ \mu\!\left(\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}E_j\right) =\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}A_k\right) =\lim_{k\to\infty}\mu(A_k) =\lim_{k\to\infty}\sum_{j=1}^{k}\mu(E_j) =\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\mu(E_j). \]

Therefore \(\mu\) is countably additive, hence a measure.

Problem 3

  1. Since

    \[ \cos x=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} \qquad\text{and}\qquad e^{-x^2/2}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{2^n n!}, \]

    and both \(\frac{x^{2n}}{(2n)!}\) and \(\frac{x^{2n}}{2^n n!}\) are decreasing when \(|x|\leq 1\), it follows that

    \[ 1-\frac{x^2}{2}\leq \cos x \leq 1-\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^4}{24}, \]

    and

    \[ e^{-x^2/2} \geq 1-\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^4}{8}-\frac{x^6}{48} \geq 1-\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^4}{8}-\frac{x^4}{12} = 1-\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^4}{24}, \]

    provided \(|x|\leq 1\). Therefore

    \[ 1-\frac{x^2}{2}\leq \cos x \leq e^{-x^2/2} \qquad (|x|\leq 1). \]

  2. Using the bounds from (a),

    \[ \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{-1}^{1}\left(1-\frac{x^2}{2}\right)^n\,dx \leq \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{-1}^{1}(\cos x)^n\,dx \leq \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{-1}^{1}e^{-n x^2/2}\,dx. \]

    For the upper bound, let \(y=\sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\,x\). Then

    \[ \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{-1}^{1}e^{-n x^2/2}\,dx = \int_{|y|\leq \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}} e^{-\pi y^2}\,dy. \]

    For the lower bound, the same substitution gives

    \[ \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{-1}^{1}\left(1-\frac{x^2}{2}\right)^n\,dx = \int_{|y|\leq \sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}} \left(1-\frac{\pi y^2}{n}\right)^n\,dy. \]

    By the Dominated Convergence Theorem, both expressions converge to

    \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\pi y^2}\,dy = 1. \]

    Therefore, by squeeze, \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt{\frac{n}{2\pi}}\int_{|x|\leq 1}(\cos x)^n\,dx=1. \]

Problem 4

Soln 1

Let \(E=\{(x,y)\in[0,1]\times[0,1]: x^a+y^b<1\}\). Note that if \[ \delta E:=\{(\delta^{1/a}x,\delta^{1/b}y):(x,y)\in E\}, \] then \[ m_2(\delta E)=\delta^{\frac1a+\frac1b}m_2(E). \]

By the layer-cake representation,

\[ \int_E \frac{1}{x^a+y^b}\,dm_2 = \int_1^{\infty} m_2\!\left(\left\{(x,y):\frac{1}{x^a+y^b}>\lambda\right\}\right)\,d\lambda = m_2(E)\int_1^{\infty}\lambda^{-(\frac1a+\frac1b)}\,d\lambda. \]

This is finite if and only if \(\frac1a+\frac1b>1\).

Soln 2

Write \([0,1]\times[0,1]=A\cup B\), where \[ A=\{(x,y): x^a\geq y^b\},\qquad B=\{(x,y): x^a<y^b\}. \] On \(A\) we have \[ x^a \leq x^a+y^b \leq 2x^a, \] so \(\frac{1}{x^a+y^b}\approx \frac{1}{x^a}\) there. Similarly, on \(B\), \(\frac{1}{x^a+y^b}\approx \frac{1}{y^b}\).

Thus it suffices to check the integrals of \(x^{-a}\) over \(A\) and \(y^{-b}\) over \(B\). By Tonelli,

\[ \int_A \frac{1}{x^a}\,dm_2 = \int_0^1\int_0^{x^{a/b}} x^{-a}\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1 x^{a/b-a}\,dx, \]

which is finite if and only if \(a/b-a>-1\), equivalently \(\frac1a+\frac1b>1\). The argument for \(\int_B \frac{1}{y^b}\,dm_2\) is the same. Hence

\[ \int_{[0,1]\times[0,1]} \frac{1}{x^a+y^b}\,dm_2(x,y)<\infty \Longleftrightarrow \frac1a+\frac1b>1. \]

Problem 5

  1. Since \(f_k\to f\) a.e., we have \(|f_k|^2\to |f|^2\) a.e. Fatou's theorem gives

    \[ \int_{\mathbb{R}} |f|^2 = \int_{\mathbb{R}} \liminf_{k\to\infty} |f_k|^2 \leq \liminf_{k\to\infty} \int_{\mathbb{R}} |f_k|^2 \leq \sup_k \|f_k\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R})}^2 < \infty. \]

    Therefore \(f\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\).

  2. Suppose \(\operatorname{supp}(g)\subset E\) with \(m(E)<\infty\). Let \(\varepsilon>0\). Since \(|g|^2\in L^1\), there exists \(\delta>0\) such that \(\int_A |g|^2<\varepsilon^2\) whenever \(m(A)<\delta\).

    By Egorov's Theorem, there exists \(F\subset E\) with \(m(E\setminus F)<\delta\) such that \(f_k\to f\) uniformly on \(F\). Hence for all sufficiently large \(k\), \(|f_k(x)-f(x)|<\varepsilon\) for every \(x\in F\), and

    \[ \left|\int_E (f_k-f)g\right| \leq \int_F |f_k-f||g| + \int_{E\setminus F} |f_k-f||g|. \]

    Using uniform convergence on \(F\) and Cauchy--Schwarz on \(E\setminus F\),

    \[ \left|\int_E (f_k-f)g\right| \leq \varepsilon \int_F |g| + \|f_k-f\|_2 \left(\int_{E\setminus F}|g|^2\right)^{1/2}. \]

    Since \(\|f_k\|_2\) is uniformly bounded and \(\|f\|_2\leq \sup_k\|f_k\|_2\) by part (a), we have \(\|f_k-f\|_2\leq 2\sup_j\|f_j\|_2\). Also \(\int_{E\setminus F}|g|^2<\varepsilon^2\). Thus \(\int_E (f_k-f)g\to 0\).

  3. Now let \(g\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) be arbitrary, and set \(g_N=g\,\chi_{\{|x|\leq N\}}\). Since \(g\in L^2\), we may choose \(N\) so that \(\|g-g_N\|_2\) is as small as we wish. Then

    \[ \left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} (f_k-f)g\right| \leq \left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} (f_k-f)g_N\right| + \|f_k-f\|_2\,\|g-g_N\|_2. \]

    The first term tends to \(0\) by the finite-measure case, and the second term can be made arbitrarily small by choosing \(N\) large and using the uniform \(L^2\)-bound on \(f_k\) and \(f\). Hence

    \[ \lim_{k\to\infty}\int_{\mathbb{R}} f_k g = \int_{\mathbb{R}} f g \]

    for all \(g\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\).
