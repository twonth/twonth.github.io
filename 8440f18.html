  • 9/6 Proof that $\sum_{p \le x} \frac{\log{p}}{p} =\log{x}+O(1)$. Abel's lemma (partial summation). Examples of rewriting sums as integrals. Demonstration that under suitable conditions, $\int_{1}^{x} o(b(t))\,dt = o(\int_{1}^{x} b(t)\, dt)$.
  • 9/4 Proof that $\psi(x) \ge (\log{2}) x + O(\log{x})$, and subsequent deduction that $\liminf_{x\to\infty} \frac{\pi(x)}{x/\log{x}} \ge \log{2}$. Observation that these bounds already imply that for each fixed $C>2$, the interval $(x,Cx]$ contains a prime for all large enough $x$. Proof that $(x,2x]$ contains primes for all large enough $x$; in fact, $\pi(2x)-\pi(x) \ge (\frac{\log{2}}{3}+o(1))\frac{x}{\log{x}}$ as $x\to\infty$.

Course summary

MATH 8440 is an introduction to analytic methods in number theory, with an emphasis on concrete, easily-described problems. Possible topics to be discussed include

  • elementary results on the distribution of prime numbers (Euclid to Euler to Chebyshev to Mertens),
  • Dirichlet's theorem on prime numbers in arithmetic progressions,
  • elementary sieve methods and their applications (e.g., Brun's theorem on twin primes, Schnirelmann’s theorem toward Goldbach’s conjecture),
  • character sums and the distribution of power residues,
  • Waring's problem,
  • the prime number theorem.
Prerequisites are minimal: I will assume familiarity with algebra and analysis (both real and complex) at the advanced undergraduate level. Prior acquaintance with elementary number theory will be helpful but is not essential.

Homework/Exams/Grading

There are no exams in this class. Your grade is based entirely on homework. If you decide to concentrate in number theory/arithmetic geometry, the material in this course may well form a part of your oral exams, and I encourage you (implore you!) to take the homework seriously.

You should expect homework assignments every one to two weeks. Each problem will carry a point value, and to receive an A in the class, you should average at least roughly half the points per assignment. Students who come to class but do not turn in homework will receive a B at the end of the term.

My policy for graduate courses is that you are free to use whatever resources you like to solve the homework problems, including textbooks, papers, and the intarwebz. I only ask that what you turn in to me is in your own words and reflects your own understanding.
