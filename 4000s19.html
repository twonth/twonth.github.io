Semester-to-date summary (reverse chronological order)
  • 4/29 For every commutative ring $R$, there is a unique homomorphism $\phi\colon \mathbb{Z} \to R$. Every finite field contains $\mathbb{Z}_p$ for some prime $p$. Every finite field has size $p^n$ for some prime $p$ and positive integer $n$. For every prime $p$ and positive integer $n$, there is an irreducible polynomial of degree $n$ over $\mathbb{Z}_p$, and hence there is a finite field of size $p^n$ (no proof). Any two finite fields of the same size are isomorphic (no proof).
  • 4/26 Further examples of computing degrees of field extensions. Introductory discussion of finite fields.
  • 4/24 More on behavior of degrees in towers of fields. Applications.
  • 4/22 Degree of $F[\alpha]$ over $F$. Degree of $F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle$ over $F$. Behavior of degrees in towers of fields (proof later). If $[K:F]=n$, then every $\alpha \in K$ is the root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$; if $p(x) \in F[x]$ is an irreducible polynomial with $p(\alpha)=0$, then $\deg{p(x)} \mid n$.
  • 4/19 Midterm #3
  • 4/17 Introduction to degree. If $F$ is a subfield of $K$, discussion of what it means for $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n \in K$ to span $K$ over $F$, to be linearly independent over $F$, and to form a basis for $K$ over $F$. Span and linearly independent $\Longleftrightarrow$ basis. Examples of bases. What it means for $K$ to have finite degree over $F$. Definition of the degree of $K$ over $F$, denoted $[K:F]$. Examples. Proof that $[K:F]=1$ $\Longleftrightarrow$ $K=F$.
  • 4/15 If $f(x)$ is a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, there is an extension $K$ of $F$ in which $f(x)$ has a root. If $f(x)$ is a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, there is an extension $K$ of $F$ over which $f(x)$ splits. Revisiting the splitting field of $x^2+1$ over $\mathbb{R}$. Uniqueness of splitting fields (not examinable).
  • 4/12Application of the FHT to understanding $F[\alpha]$. The evaluation homomorphism $\mathrm{ev}_{\alpha}\colon F[x] \to F[\alpha]$. If $\alpha$ is the root of an irreducible polynomial $p(x) \in F[x]$, then $F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle \cong F[\alpha]$. If $\alpha$ is not the root of any nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $F[x] \cong F[\alpha]$. Start of discussion of constructing splitting fields.
  • 4/10 Review of the isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_5$. Proof there is no isomorphism between $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ and $\mathbb{Z}_4$ (by counting zero divisors). Detailed proof of the FHT.
  • 4/8 Review of the definition of an isomorphism. Definition of the direct product $R\times S$. Example of $\mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_5$. A possible isomorphism between $\mathbb{Z}_3\times \mathbb{Z}_5$ and $\mathbb{Z}_{15}$. Setup for the fundamental homomorphism theorem (FHT). Proof, assuming FHT, that $\mathbb{Z}_{15} \cong \mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_5$.
  • 4/5 More about computing in $F[x]/\langle f(x)\rangle$. Example of $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle$, and realization this is somehow the same as $\mathbb{C}$. Definition of an isomorphism, and when two rings are isomorphic. Isomorphism is an equivalence relation on the class of rings. A homomorphism of rings is injective $\Longleftrightarrow$ kernel = $\{0\}$. Detailed proof that complex conjugation is an isomorphism of $\mathbb{C}$ with itself.
  • 4/3 Proof that the "reduction mod $I$" map from $R$ to $R/I$ sending $a$ to $\bar{a}$ is a homomorphism with kernel $I$. Start of discussion of $F[x]/\langle f(x)\rangle$. Description of elements of $F[x]/\langle f(x)\rangle$. The four-element field $\mathbb{Z}_2[x]/\langle x^2+x+1\rangle$. Observation that $\mathbb{Z}_3[x]/\langle x^2\rangle$ is not a field.

HW: Every ideal in $F[x]$ is principal, and the same for all ideals of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Given a ring $R$ an ideal $I$ of $R$, the construction of the quotient ring $R/I$.

More generally, if $f(x) \in F[x]$ is nonconstant, and $K$ is a field over which $f(x)$ splits, say $f(x) = A(x-r_1)\cdots (x-r_d)$, then $F[r_1,\dots,r_d]$ is a splitting field for $f(x)$ over $F$. The splitting field of $x^2+x+1$ over $\mathbb{Z}_2$.
  • 3/18 Review of known results about $F[\alpha]$. Definition of what it means to say $f(x) \in F[x]$ splits over $K$, where $K$ is an extension field of $F$. Definition of what it means to say that $K$ is a splitting field of $f(x)$ over $F$. Examples of splitting fields.
  • 3/8 Definition of the terms subfield and field extension. Definition of $F[\alpha]$, when $F$ is a subfield of $K$ and $\alpha \in K$. $F[\alpha]$ is always a subring of $K$. If $\alpha$ is a root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $F[\alpha]$ is a subfield of $K$. If $\alpha \in K$ is the root of a degree $n$ irreducible polynomial $p(x) \in F[x]$, then $F[\alpha] = \{a_0 + a_1\alpha + \dots + a_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1}: a_0, a_1, \ldots \in F\}$. Moreover (HW), each element of $F[\alpha]$ has a unique representation in the form $a_0 + a_1 \alpha + \dots + a_{n-1} \alpha^{n-1}$ with the $a_i \in F$. Extended example of $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]$.
  • 3/6 gcds exist in $F[x]$ and can be computed by Euclid's algorithm. Example. Uniqueness of gcd up to multiplication by constant factors (HW). Definition of irreducibles (primes) in $F[x]$. Linear polynomials are irreducible, and degree $2$ and $3$ polynomials are irreducible $\Longleftrightarrow$ they have no root in $F$ (HW). Statement of unique factorization; sketch of the proof.

Examples of division. The remainder theorem and root factor theorem in $F[x]$. Definition of the gcd in $F[x]$.
  • 3/1 The magic substitution $w=v-\frac{p}{3v}$. An example of solving the cubic. Definition of polynomials over an arbitrary commutative ring $R$, and $R[x]$.
  • 2/27 Examples of computing $n$th roots. The $n$th roots of $1$ are the vertices of a regular $n$-gon. Solving linear and quadratic equations over $\mathbb{C}$. Reduction of solving cubics to solving cubics of the special form $w^3+pw+q=0$.

  • 2/20 Reminder of intermediate value theorem. Construction of the complex numbers via ordered pairs, and check that in this way we obtain a field containing $\mathbb{R}$ and also a square root of $-1$ (but *not* with an ordering preserving that on $\mathbb{R}$). Statement of Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. Brief discussion of viewing complex numbers geometrically.
  • 2/18 Wrap up of discussion of $\mathbb{Q}$. Motivation for wanting a system of numbers that "fills in the holes" of $\mathbb{Q}$. Precise statement (in terms of bounded increasing sequences converging). Statement that such a system $\mathbb{R}$ exists and is unique.

Every element of $\mathbb{Q}$ is expressible as $a\cdot b^{-1}$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$. Definition of a positive set for an integral domain. Proof that $\mathbb{Q}^{+}$, defined as the set of $a/b$ with $ab>0$, is a positive set for $\mathbb{Q}$. Properties of inequalities for any domain with a given positive set.
  • 2/11 Definition of the rational numbers as equivalence classes of ordered pairs of integers, under cross-multiplication equivalence. $\mathbb{Q}$ is a commutative ring. $\mathbb{Q}$ is a field. Discussion of what it means for $\mathbb{Q}$ to contain $\mathbb{Z}$.

  • 2/6 Check that $+$ and $\cdot$ are well-defined on $\mathbb{Z}_m$. Verification that $\mathbb{Z}_m$ has all the properties down through D1 from the initial handout. Definition of a ring and a commutative ring. A ring where $1=0$ consists solely of $0$. $\mathbb{Z}_3$ vs $\mathbb{Z}_4$ and the definition of an integral domain. Conjecture that $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is an integral domain if and only if $m$ is a prime number.
  • 2/4 Describing all solutions in the Chinese remainder theorem. Examples. Definition of $\mathbb{Z}_m$, and of addition and multiplication on $\mathbb{Z}_m$.
  • 2/1 If $\gcd(a,m)=1$, then there is an integer $d$ with $ad\equiv 1\pmod{m}$. If $\gcd(a,m)=1$, then there is a unique mod $m$ solution to $ax \equiv b\pmod{m}$. If $\gcd(a,m) =d$, then $ax\equiv b\pmod{m}$ has no solution if $d\not\mid b$; if $d\mid b$, it reduces to $\frac{a}{d}x\equiv \frac{b}{d} \pmod{\frac{m}{d}}$. Statement and proof of the Chinese remainder theorem.
  • 1/30 Every integer is congruent, mod $m$, to exactly one of $0,1,2\dots,m-1$. $N$ is divisible by $9$ $\Longleftrightarrow$ the sum of its digits is divisible by $9$. Fermat's little theorem, statement and proof (using binomial coefficients). The sequence $2,5,8,\dots$ contains no squares (by arguing mod $3$).

Course synopsis

At this point in your mathematical career, you have accumulated a wealth of experience computing with integers, real numbers, and complex numbers. These items are so familiar to you that they may even have the appearance of being God-given --- as if understanding those objects is What Mathematics is All About.

Abstract algebra challenges this notion. Integers, real numbers, and complex numbers are indeed fantastic, but they are fantastic not because they are handed down from on high, but because they have a rich and (presumably) consistent theory with useful consequences. And given how useful these objects are, we are compelled to isolate (and consider in the abstract) their most important properties. Once we do so, we find that there are many other objects with the same sorts of interesting properties. For example, we will see that the integers are an example of what is called a ring, while the real numbers and complex numbers are fields. These more abstract-seeming objects are not just interesting in an intellectual let's-talk-about-this-over-coffee kind of way, but understanding them deeply often leads to a new understanding of the objects of original interest.

Let me give one concrete example: Some primes, like 5, can be written as a sum of two squares: $5 = 1^2 + 2^2$. And other primes, like 3, cannot. The question of when this is possible is a question about the integers. But our answer to this question --- and we will answer it by the end of the semester --- will require us to visit a totally different mathematically system, the ring of Gaussian integers.

No make-up exams will be given. The final exam is cumulative. Your grade is made up of the following weighted components:

  • Each midterm: 15% (total of 45%)
  • Homework: 25%
  • Final exam: 30%

This class falls into the interactive lecture genre (not entirely unrelated to the practice of call and response in a liturgical context). What this means is that I intend to punctuate the lectures frequently with questions for you. For the show to go on, class participation is absolutely essential. Since you cannot participate in class if you are not present in class, your attendance is required. In particular, more than four unexcused absences may result in you being automagically withdrawn from the class. Of course, missing class is sometimes a necessity; keep me posted whenever you have a conflict and we should not have any issues.

Homework will be collected roughly once each week. As a general rule, late homework assignments are not accepted. Your lowest HW score will be dropped at the end of the semester.

All exams are closed book and closed notes.

Students enrolled in MATH 6000 will take the same exams as the students in MATH 4000 but will be assigned additional homework problems.

You are not only allowed, but encouraged to collaborate with your classmates on the homework assignments. The joy of mathematical discovery was meant to be shared! Having said that, collaboration does not mean copying. You may not copy solutions from a textbook, classmate, website, etcetera, and you must be the one to handwrite (or type) your solutions. By entering UGA, you have already agreed to abide by the UGA honor code described in detail at https://honesty.uga.edu/Academic-Honesty-Policy/. A good rule of thumb is that you should be able to explain any work you turn in to a hypothetical interrogator.
