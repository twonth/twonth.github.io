Semester-to-date summary (reverse chronological order)
  • 11/29 Proof that there is a unique homomorphism from $\mathbb{Z}$ to any commutative ring. Any finite field contains a copy of $\mathbb{Z}_p$. Every finite field has $p^n$ elements for some prime $p$ and some positive integer $n$. For each prime $p$ and positive integer $n$, there is a finite field of size $p^n$ (look at roots of $x^{p^n}-x$ in some extension of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ where $x^{p^n}-x$ splits). Sketch of proof that any two finite fields of the same size are isomorphic.
  • 11/27 Review of definitions and theorems from last class around degrees of field extensions. Examples. If $L/F$ has degree $n$, then every $\alpha \in L$ is the root of an irreducible polynomial in $F[x]$ of degree dividing $n$. Beginning of discussion of finite fields. Examples. Exploration of possible sizes of finite fields.
  • 11/15 Midterm #3.
  • 11/13 Spanning, linear independence, and bases in the context of field extensions. Examples of bases. Definition of an extension of finite degree. Existence of bases for exensions of finite degree; spanning sets contain bases; linearly independent sets can be extended to bases; any two bases have the same number of elements. Definition of the degree of a field extension. If $F$ is a subfield of $K$, and $\alpha \in K$ is the root of an irreducible $p(x) \in F[x]$, then $[F[\alpha]:F]=\deg p(x)$. Behavior of the degree in a tower of fields.
  • 11/8 The evaluation map $\mathrm{ev}_{\alpha}\colon F[x]\to K$ and its kernel; application of the Fundamental Homomorphism Theorem to this situation. Examples. Proof that no prime $p\equiv 1\pmod{4}$ is prime in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$; deduction that all such primes are sums of two squares in $\mathbb{Z}$.
  • 11/6 Definition of isomorphism. Examples. Definition of the direct product of rings. The fundamental homomorphism theorem. The souped up Chinese remainder: If $\gcd(m,n)=1$, then $\mathbb{Z}_{mn} \cong \mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_{n}$.
  • 11/1 Quotient rings of $F[x]$. A description of the elements of $F[x]/\langle m(x)\rangle$. When is $F[x]/\langle m(x)\rangle$ a domain? a field? Every nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$ has a splitting field. Identification of $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle$ with $\mathbb{C}$.
  • 10/30 Definition of an ideal. Finitely generated ideals: $\langle r_1, \dots, r_k\rangle$. Every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is generated by a single element. The same for $F[x]$ and $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Question: Is every ideal the kernel of some homomorphism? Construction of the quotient ring $R/I$. The homomorphism $R \to R/I$ mapping $a\mapsto \bar{a}$ is a homomorphism with kernel $I$. (So YES, every ideal is the kernel of some homomorphism.)
  • 10/25 Proving irreducibility by reduction mod $p$: A general theorem. Eisenstein's irreducibility criterion, with examples. Definition of a ring homomorphism, and examples. Definition of the kernel of a homomorphism. $0$ is an element of the kernel for every homomorphism. The kernel of a homomorphism is closed under addition and absorbs multiplication.
  • 10/23 Midterm #2.
  • 10/18 If $F$ is a field and $f(x) \in F[x]$ is nonconstant, and there is a field containing $F$ in which $f(x)$ splits, then $f(x)$ has a splitting field over $F$: namely, the field obtained by adjoining all of the roots of $f(x)$ to $F$. Statement (but no proof) that if $F$ is a field and $f(x) \in F[x]$ is any nonconstant polynomial, then there is a splitting field for $f(x)$ over $F$. Rational root test. Gauss's lemma (no proof). Example of proving irreducibility by reducing mod $p$.
  • 10/16 If $F$ is a subfield of $K$, and $\alpha \in K$ is a root of a nonconstant polynomial in $F[x]$, then $F[\alpha]$ is a field. If $F$ is a subfield of $K$, and $\alpha \in K$ is a root of an irreducible polynomial $p(x) \in F[x]$ of degree $n$, then every element of $F[\alpha]$ is uniquely expressible in the form $c_0 + c_1 \alpha + \dots + c_{n-1} \alpha^{n-1}$, where $c_0, c_1, ..., c_{n-1} \in F$. Definition of what it means to say a polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ splits over an extension field $K$. Definition of what it means to say $K$ is a splitting field for $f(x)$ over $F$. Examples.
  • 10/9 Review of definition of gcd in $F[x]$, definition of irreducibles. Statement of UFT in $F[x]$. Euclid's lemma. Sketch of proof of UFT for $F[x]$. Definition of subfield, field extension. Definition of $F[\alpha]$. Study of $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]$ --- description of all elements and proof that $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt{2}]$ is a field.
  • 10/2 Statement of the fundamental theorem of algebra. Definition of $R[x]$. Degrees of polynomials. If $R$ is a domain, then $\deg{fg}=\deg{f}+\deg{g}$; as a consequence, $R[x]$ is a domain. If $F$ is a field, there is a division algorithm in $F[x]$. Definition of the gcd in $F[x]$, and calculation of the gcd with Euclid's algorithm. Definition of prime/irreducible polynomials in $F[x]$.
  • 9/27 Summary of $n$th roots. Solving linear equations, quadratic equations, and cubic equations over the complex numbers.
  • 9/25 Polar form of complex numbers. Multiplication of complex numbers in polar form. de Moivre's theorem. Every complex number has an $n$th root. $1$ has $n$ different complex $n$th roots. Understanding all $n$th roots of a given complex number. Examples.
  • 9/20 Midterm \#1
  • 9/18 Example illustrating that "holes" in the real numbers come from failure of the monotone convergence theorem. Revised wish list for $\mathbb{R}$. Statement of existence and uniqueness of $\mathbb{R}$. Construction of $\mathbb{C}$ from $\mathbb{R}$, and verification that $\mathbb{C}$ is an ordered field containing $\mathbb{R}$ and an element $i$ with $i^2=-1$. Definitions of conjugate, real part, imaginary part, polar form.
  • 9/13 Def. of an ordering on a domain $R$, and of an ordered domain. Some universal properties of ordered domains. In an ordered field, for any $\alpha,\beta$ with $\alpha < \beta$, there is a $\gamma$ with $\alpha < \gamma < \beta$. In $\mathbb{Q}$, there is no $x$ with $x^2=2$. Brief discussion of what we want from the real numbers.
  • 9/11 Definition of $\mathbb{Q}$ as fractions $a/b$ (integers, with $b$ nonzero), up to cross-multiplication equivalence. Definitions of addition and multiplication on $\mathbb{Q}$, and proof that these are well-defined. Verification that $\mathbb{Q}$ is a field in which $\mathbb{Z}$ "lives inside", and that every element of $\mathbb{Q}$ has the form $ab^{-1}$ with $a,b$ from (our copy of) $\mathbb{Z}$, and $b\ne 0$. Brief discussion of the quotient field/fraction field of an integral domain.
  • 9/6 Definition of multiplicative inverses; examples. Definition of a field. Every field is an integral domain. $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is an integral domain $\Longleftrightarrow$ $m$ is prime. Every finite integral domain is a field. If $a^{100}=0$ in a ring $R$, then $1+a$ is a unit in $R$.
  • 9/4 Definition of $\mathbb{Z}_m$, as well as definitions of $+$ and $\cdot$ on $\mathbb{Z}_m$. Proof that these operations are well-defined. Definition of (commutative, and not-necessarily commutative) rings; spot check that $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is a commutative ring. The binomial theorem holds in any commutative ring. Comparison of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ vs $\mathbb{Z}_4$, and the definition of a domain. Proof that for $m>1$, we have $\mathbb{Z}_m$ is a domain $\Longleftrightarrow$ $m$ is a prime number.
  • 8/30 Discussion of cancellation in congruences. If $\gcd(c,m)=1$, then $1/c$ exist mod $m$. Solving linear congruences. Chinese remainder theorem. Numerical examples.
  • 8/28 Every integer is congruent, mod $m$, to exactly one of $0,1,2,\dots,m-1$. Congruence mod $m$ is an equivalence relation. Can add and multiply congruences: If $a\equiv b\pmod{m}$ and $c\equiv d\pmod{m}$, then $a+c \equiv b+d\pmod{m}$, and $ac\equiv bd\pmod{m}$. Numerical examples. $N \equiv \text{(digit sum)}\pmod{9}$. No squares are $\equiv 2\pmod{3}$. For primes $p$, all middle binomial coefficients $p \choose k$ (with $0 < k < p$) are divisible by $p$. Fermat's little theorem.
  • 8/23Proof Euclid's Algorithm works. $\gcd(a,b)=ax+by$ for some $x,y \in \mathbb{Z}$ (proved in HW). Fundamental lemma: if $a\mid bc$ and $\gcd(a,b)=1$, then $a\mid c$. Every integer $>1$ can be factored as a product of primes. Euclid's lemma: If $p\mid ab$, then $p\mid a$ or $p\mid b$. Factorization into primes is unique. Definition of congruences.
  • 8/21Proof of binomial theorem. Division algorithm and application to even/odd numbers. Definition of "$b$ divides $a$". Basic properties of divisibility. Definition of gcd and examples. Euclid's algorithm. Start of justification of Euclid's algorithm.
  • 8/16Proof that $a\cdot 0 =0$. $a(-1)=-a$. Laws governing inequalities. $1$ is the least element of $\mathbb{Z}^{+}$. Induction as a consequence of the Well Ordering Principle. Laws of exponents. Lead-in to the binomial theorem.
  • 8/14Review of syllabus. Fundamental properties of $\mathbb{Z}$. Proofs from axioms: the additive and multiplicative identities are unique, and additive inverses are unique. Start of discussion of $a\cdot 0 =0$.
Course synopsis

At this point in your mathematical career, you have accumulated a wealth of experience computing with integers, real numbers, and complex numbers. These items are so familiar to you that they may even have the appearance of being God-given --- as if understanding those objects is What Mathematics is All About.

Abstract algebra challenges this notion. Integers, real numbers, and complex numbers are indeed fantastic, but they are fantastic not because they are handed down from on high, but because they have a rich and (presumably) consistent theory with useful consequences. And given how useful these objects are, we are compelled to isolate (and consider in the abstract) their most important properties. Once we do so, we find that there are many other objects with the same sorts of interesting properties. For example, we will see that the integers are an example of what is called a ring, while the real numbers and complex numbers are fields. These more abstract-seeming objects are not just interesting in an intellectual let's-talk-about-this-over-coffee kind of way, but understanding them deeply often leads to a new understanding of the objects of original interest.

Let me give one concrete example: Some primes, like 5, can be written as a sum of two squares: $5 = 1^2 + 2^2$. And other primes, like 3, cannot. The question of when this is possible is a question about the integers. But our answer to this question --- and we will answer it by the end of the semester --- will require us to visit a totally different mathematically system, the ring of Gaussian integers.

No make-up exams will be given. The final exam is cumulative. Your grade is made up of the following weighted components:

  • Each midterm: 15% (total of 45%)
  • Homework: 25%
  • Final exam: 30%

This class falls into the interactive lecture genre (not entirely unrelated to the practice of call and response in a liturgical context). What this means is that I intend to punctuate the lectures frequently with questions for you. For the show to go on, class participation is absolutely essential. Since you cannot participate in class if you are not present in class, your attendance is required. In particular, more than four unexcused absences may result in you being automagically withdrawn from the class. Of course, missing class is sometimes a necessity; keep me posted whenever you have a conflict and we should not have any issues.

Homework will be collected roughly once each week. As a general rule, late homework assignments are not accepted. Your lowest HW score will be dropped at the end of the semester.

All exams are closed book and closed notes.

Students enrolled in MATH 6000 will take the same exams as the students in MATH 4000 but will be assigned additional homework problems.

You are not only allowed, but encouraged to collaborate with your classmates on the homework assignments. The joy of mathematical discovery was meant to be shared! Having said that, collaboration does not mean copying. You may not copy solutions from a textbook, classmate, website, etcetera, and you must be the one to handwrite (or type) your solutions. By entering UGA, you have already agreed to abide by the UGA honor code described in detail at https://honesty.uga.edu/Academic-Honesty-Policy/. A good rule of thumb is that you should be able to explain any work you turn in to a hypothetical interrogator.
