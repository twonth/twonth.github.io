Course summary

This course is an introduction to arithmetic beyond $\mathbb{Z}$.

To give you just the briefest idea of what this means, you have surely heard of the fundamental theorem of arithmetic: every integer not $0$ or $\pm 1$ can be factored uniquely as a product of primes. Possibly you learned in your first algebra course that the analogous result holds if we adjoin a square root of $-1$ to $\mathbb{Z}$, but not if we adjoin a square root of $-5$.

Less well known is the following: Despite the failure of unique factorization in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, any two factorizations of the same element of $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{−5}]$ have the same length. In other words, while the factorizations themselves can be different, the number of irreducibles involved cannot. It turns out that all of these phenomena concerning elementwise factorization can be explained by looking at factorizations of ideals. We will spend a large portion of the course developing this theory, which was pioneered by Dedekind and Kummer in the latter half of the 19th century. The scope of this theory extends far beyond $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{−5}]$; a satisfactory theory of factorization of ideals into prime ideals can be developed in the "ring of integers" of any "number field".

The tools you will be introduced to in this course are part of the standard tool chest of every working number theorist. As such, this course is strongly recommended for anyone pursuing further studies in number theory.

We will aim to cover the standard core topics in a first course in algebraic number theory:

  1. Basic concepts: Norms, traces, discriminants
  2. Arithmetic in the ring of integers of a number field and the "fundamental theorem of ideal theory"
  3. Factoring of prime ideals in extensions
  4. Finiteness of the ideal class group
  5. Dirichlet's theorem on the structure of the unit group
  6. Special examples: Quadratic and cyclotomic fields
  7. Factoring of primes in Galois extensions
  8. Statement of algebro-analytic results (including Chebotarev's density theorem)
  9. Other topics as time permits

For the most part, lectures will follow my AMS text. However, we will sometimes wish to discuss topics not treated there, and Samuel's text will be our go-to reference.

There are several possible approaches to algebraic number theory; I recommend browsing the QA247 stacks and sampling other texts. Some that are certainly worth a look: Alaca and Williams, Ash, Cox, Fröhlich and Taylor, Hecke, Ireland and Rosen, Jarvis, Lang, Lorenzini, Marcus, Stewart and Tall.

Homework/Exams/Grading

There are no exams in this class. Your grade is based entirely on homework. If you decide to concentrate in number theory/arithmetic geometry, the material in this course may well form a part of your oral exams, and I encourage you (implore you!) to take the homework seriously.

You should expect homework assignments every one to two weeks. Each problem will carry a point value, and to receive an A in the class, you should average at least roughly half the points per assignment. Students who come to class but do not turn in homework will receive a B at the end of the term.

My policy for graduate courses is that you are free to use whatever resources you like to solve the homework problems, including textbooks, papers, and the intarwebz. I only ask that what you turn in to me is in your own words and reflects your own understanding.
