Course synopsis

MATH 3220 is all about "problem solving" strategies. In this sense, it's different than most other math classes at UGA which focus on developing individual subject areas. We will dip our toes into several different subjects --- never going very deep, but picking up the basics. The material we cover should be applicable to whatever area of mathematics that you end up pursuing --- and even in quantitative fields outside of pure mathematics. Many of the problems we discuss require virtually no background to state, despite possessing fiendishly difficult solutions. A not-so-secret goal of this class is to help prepare you for the annual William Lowell Putnam Competition, a six-hour national mathematics competition for undergraduates.

Our time together will be devoted to collective, collaborative problem solving. Most of how the class runs is up to you, and I will rely on you for feedback throughout the semester about what is working and what isn't. A typical class could consist of a mini-lecture by me, forty minutes or so of problem solving among yourselves, and then mini lectures from y'all presenting solutions to your favorite problems.

Since we meet only an hour per week, class attendance is required. Please contact me in advance if you have a conflict that will require you to miss a session.

Both of these books discuss problem solving strategies, with a number of worked examples (and a number of examples left to you to work!). I'm more familiar with Larson's book, but the text of Gelca and Andreescu also looks very nice. Many of the examples on our problem worksheets will be taken from these books.

Homework/ Exams/ Grading

The grading for this class is Satisfactory/Unsatisfactory. To receive an S in the course, you must attend class faithfully and must write-up solutions to at least five approved problems over the course of the semester. These approved problems may come from problem sets that I hand out in class or copies of old Putnam exams.

There will be no in-class exams and no final exam.

I would strongly encourage you to work with others on the problems, and to share your ideas and strategies with your classmates --- both in and out of class. As iron sharpens iron, so one man (or woman!) sharpens another.

However, please make sure that you understand everything you turn in; if requested, you should be able to verbally explain your solutions to me. While it is OK to write-up problems whose solutions we discuss in class, you are not allowed to turn in a solution based on something you looked up online.
